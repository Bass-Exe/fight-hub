FOLDER 1 — KRONK

Kronk Protocol

Thomas "Hitman" Hearns & Anthony "The Magician" Sims Jr. · Kronk Gym, Detroit

The Kronk style was built in the basement of a Detroit rec center under trainer Emanuel Steward — gold and red trunks, a sweatbox gym, and one philosophy: control range with the jab, load every cross with knockout power, and never stand still long enough to get hit back. It turned Hearns into a five-division world champion, and it still shapes fighters today through the Kronk lineage, including modern light heavyweight Anthony Sims Jr.

02 · LOAD EVERY CROSSIf it's not going to hurt, don't throw it.
03 · NEVER REPEATThe same combo twice means they've solved you.

BASE STANCE — RANGE & REACH

FOOT POSITION — TOP VIEW

GUARD — FRONT VIEW

FEET

  • Weight close to even at rest, riding toward the back foot (about 55%) when probing range with the jab.

HANDS

  • Lead hand active and slightly extended rather than glued to the cheek — always ready to measure range.
  • Rear hand tucked tight at the cheekbone, elbow protecting the ribs. Chin tucked behind the lead shoulder.
BOUT 1 — RANGE WAR

The Pawing Jab & Stiff-Arm Control

Thomas "Hitman" Hearns · Kronk Gym, Detroit

Hearns' jab wasn't just a punch that scored points — it was a steering wheel. He used a long, pawing jab to find distance, a quicker flicker jab to freeze opponents and break their rhythm, and at times turned the same lead hand into a stiff-arm or collar-tie to control an opponent's head and balance. The jab decides everything that comes after it. Master this and the cross becomes a formality.

FEET

  • Stand slightly taller than your usual stance — Hearns used his height, you use your reach.
  • Lead foot points straight at the target. Back foot turned out ~45°, stance a touch wider than normal to support a long, extended jab.
  • Weight rides 55% on the back foot at range — this lets the front foot fire and recover without dragging your balance forward.
  • Push the jab off the ball of the back foot, never the heel.

HANDS

  • Lead hand floats slightly more extended than a normal guard — active and out in space, not glued to the cheek.
  • Rear hand stays tight at the cheekbone, elbow tucked to the ribs. This is your insurance while the lead hand works.
  • Pawing jab: extend slow, fingers loosely open like you're measuring distance, snap shut only the last few inches.
  • Flicker jab: same line, but fast and short — just enough to block vision and break timing.
  • Stiff-arm: extend the jab and instead of fully retracting, rest the glove on the target's shoulder line for half a second before pulling back.

BOUT 2 — THE ANGLE

Pivot Power & The Never-Repeat Combo

Anthony "The Magician" Sims Jr. · Kronk Lineage

Sims Jr. earned the nickname "The Magician" because opponents could never predict where the next shot was coming from. He generates knockout power from angles most fighters don't bother to find — pivoting off a jab instead of standing and trading, switching his combination mid-stream so the fourth punch never matches what the first three suggested. The lesson: power isn't just hip rotation, it's hip rotation from an angle your opponent didn't see you create.

FEET

  • Start square in your normal stance, but treat your lead foot as a pivot point, not just an anchor.
  • After your jab lands, pivot on the ball of the lead foot 30–45° — this rotates your whole body off the line your opponent is aiming at.
  • Rear foot follows the pivot, replanting to re-establish a full stance at the new angle.
  • Stay on the balls of both feet through the pivot — flat feet can't turn fast enough.

HANDS

  • Hands stay exactly where they are during the pivot. The angle change comes from your feet and hips — not from dropping the guard to "help" you turn.
  • Load power into the rear hand by coiling the rear hip and shoulder back slightly before you throw. Bigger coil, bigger snap.
  • After any 2–3 punch combo, the next punch should never match the pattern your opponent just saw. Threw jab-cross-hook? Don't follow with hook again — switch to an uppercut or a body shot from the new angle.

BOUT 3 — THE FINISH

The Chopping Cross & Full Fight Simulation

Hearns' Finisher + Sims' Angles, Combined

Hearns' jab created the opening — but the chopping right cross is what ended fights. Instead of a flat, straight cross, he threw his right hand on a slight downward angle, chopping over a guard that had been lowered or distracted by the jab. Combine that finishing power with Sims Jr.'s habit of creating an angle first, and you get the full Kronk formula: control range, create an angle, then chop the finishing shot down through the opening. Today you put the whole week together.

FEET

  • Same base stance — but as you throw the chopping cross, drive up and slightly forward off the back foot. Power comes from the floor up, not just the shoulder.
  • If you've pivoted first (Thursday's skill), the chopping cross now arrives from an angle your opponent isn't braced for. That's the whole point of stacking these two days.

HANDS

  • The chopping cross doesn't travel flat — it arcs slightly downward, like you're throwing it OVER something rather than through it.
  • Drop the rear shoulder slightly as you load, then drive up and over as you release. Your fist finishes higher than where the target's chin started — you've come down on top of it.
  • Lead hand stays up and tight to your own chin the instant you commit to the cross. This is the one punch where over-committing gets you caught.

LENNOX LEWIS · KRONK The Power Jab
▾

Most fighters treat the jab as a tool to measure distance. Steward took Lewis's ordinary jab and turned it into a weapon on its own — thrown with real force, not just to set up something else. Paired with a heavy straight right, it became one of the most damaging one-twos in the sport, capable of hurting an opponent before the right hand ever lands.

FEET

  • Same orthodox base, but plant slightly heavier on the lead foot as you throw — you're driving through the target, not just touching it.
  • Back foot stays grounded to anchor the rotation. This isn't a step-in jab, it's a loaded one from your existing range.

HANDS

  • Rotate the lead shoulder and hip into the punch the way you would a cross — it should carry the same intent as your power shots.
  • Snap the fist at full extension with real speed, then follow immediately with a straight right on the same line.

KRONK DEFENSE · TRICK The Check Hook
▾

When an opponent surges forward, most fighters retreat straight back — exactly what an aggressive fighter wants, since it keeps them in a straight line to chase. The check hook does the opposite: instead of retreating, you pivot off the lead foot and throw a hook as they step in, using their own momentum against them while you land on an angle they never saw coming.

FEET

  • As the opponent steps forward, pivot your lead foot roughly 90° rather than stepping backward.
  • Rear foot follows the pivot, landing you completely off their forward line — you end up almost beside them, not behind your old spot.

HANDS

  • Throw the lead hook AS you pivot, not before or after — the hook and the foot pivot happen on the same beat.
  • Keep the rear hand glued to your chin through the whole movement; you're moving past their power hand, not away from it.

GERALD McCLELLAN · KRONK The G-Man Hook
▾

Gerald McClellan's left hook was one of the most feared single punches the Kronk gym ever produced — a short, violent shot built from technique and timing as much as raw power, capable of changing a fight in an instant. The lesson isn't "swing harder," it's "transfer cleaner."

FEET

  • Same base stance — the hook starts in your back foot's toes, pivoting the same direction as the punch.
  • Weight transfers fully onto the front foot as the hook lands. Don't leave it split between both feet — that's where the power leaks out.

HANDS

  • Keep the hook short — elbow at roughly 90°, fist traveling in a tight arc, not a wide swing.
  • Power comes from hip and shoulder rotation snapping through; the arm is along for the ride. Rear hand stays at the chin the entire time.

KRONK FINISHER · BODY ATTACK The Shovel Hook
▾

A shovel hook sits between a hook and an uppercut — a short, compact punch thrown at roughly a 45° angle into the body or chin. It's most effective as a surprise, landed after the opponent's eyes have been pulled upstairs by a jab and cross. Set it up right and it's one of the cleanest ways to finish a round.

FEET

  • Bend the knees slightly to drop your level before throwing, then drive upward through the legs as you release — power comes from underneath, not from reaching down with the arm.
  • Rear foot pivots slightly inward as you throw, same as a hook.

HANDS

  • Set it up behind a jab-cross — by the third punch their eyes are upstairs, not guarding the body.
  • Throw at a 45° angle, scooping upward and across at once. Aim for the liver, or the chin if the body's too well covered.

HILMER KENTY · KRONK ORIGINAL The Flurry Jab
▾

Hilmer Kenty was the first world champion to come out of the Kronk gym, and he got there on a jab thrown in fast, repeated bursts rather than single shots — overwhelming opponents with volume and rhythm before they could find their own range.

FEET

  • Stay light and bouncy rather than planted — this is a volume technique, your feet need to keep resetting between jabs.
  • Small forward steps with each jab in the burst, not one big step. Rapid small adjustments, not a lunge.

HANDS

  • Throw 3–4 jabs in a fast burst rather than one at a time — the first finds range, the rest overwhelm.
  • Keep every jab full speed. Rear hand never drops during the flurry, even though it's tempting to load it for a finisher.

KRONK COUNTER · TRICK The Pull-Counter Right
▾

Rather than blocking or slipping an incoming jab, the pull-counter uses a slight backward lean from the waist to let the punch fall just short — then answers immediately with a straight right while the opponent is still extended and out of position to defend.

FEET

  • Weight shifts onto the back foot as you pull your head back, but keep both feet on the ground — this is a lean, not a retreat.
  • Stay ready to drive forward off the back foot the instant you counter; the pull only works if the answer comes back fast.

HANDS

  • Pull back just enough for the jab to miss by an inch or two — too far costs you the range to counter effectively.
  • Fire the straight right the moment their arm is fully extended. Lead hand stays up near your face the whole time.

FOLDER 2 — SUGAR RAY

Sugar Ray Leonard

Five-Division World Champion · Boxer-Puncher · One of the Four Kings

Sugar Ray Leonard built a career on a simple principle: he never let a fight stay one thing. Against Roberto Duran the first time, he tried to box and got bullied. In the rematch, he danced and clowned and made Duran quit. Against Thomas Hearns, he fell behind on the cards through twelve rounds of being outboxed — then in round thirteen Angelo Dundee told him he was blowing it, and Leonard walked through Hearns in thirteen and stopped him in fourteen. Against Marvin Hagler, he moved for eleven rounds, traded for one, then moved again. Every time the game changed against him, he had another gear. That adaptability is the whole Leonard curriculum.

Underneath the showmanship — the bolo punch, the Ali Shuffle, the taunting — was real, hard technique. His jab did more work than any other punch in his arsenal, used at least four different ways depending on what the moment needed. His footwork wasn't just movement; it was his offense, setting angles before a punch ever landed. He would slide in closer with two or three quick movements in his opponent's direction to force a defensive posture — or cause them to fire a shot he was prepared to slip and counter. Everything in this folder connects back to those two things: the jab and the feet that make it work.

01 · THE JAB IS YOUR EVERYTHINGIt controls range, sets up power, and scores on its own.
02 · FEET BEFORE FISTSGet your feet in the right place before you throw anything.
03 · CHANGE THE GAMEIf what you're doing isn't working, do something else — right now.

BASE STANCE — THE BOXER-PUNCHER

FOOT POSITION — TOP VIEW

GUARD — FRONT VIEW

FEET

  • Orthodox, shoulder-width — but always live. Leonard almost never planted for more than two consecutive punches. His feet reset between every combination.
  • Weight stays close to 50/50, which gives him the ability to spring forward into power mode OR retreat into pure boxing without needing to shift his base first. Duran beat him the first time partly by forcing him to fight flat-footed against the ropes.
  • Knees slightly bent, never locked. His lead knee had a constant, very subtle flex — you can see it in footage. It's what lets him change direction instantly.
  • He always moves his lead foot first — whether stepping in, out, or laterally. The back foot follows, never crosses.

HANDS

  • High guard, both hands near the face, but the lead hand is always in motion — floating, feinting, measuring. Never glued. It becomes the jab, the bolo setup, the body feint.
  • Rear hand locked at the right cheekbone, elbow in tight to the ribs. Stays there until it's time to fire — then it explodes, then it comes straight back. No lingering.
  • Chin is tucked behind the lead shoulder, not just dropped. Watch his fights — his head is almost always slightly angled so the lead shoulder is between his chin and his opponent.
  • Eyes stay up and tracking the center of his opponent's chest, not their hands. Watching hands is a beginner's habit. Chest movement tells you what's coming before the gloves move.

JAB MASTERCLASS + FOOTWORK

Leonard almost always used the jab as the initiator for any attack — but he varied the level of the target to confuse opponents. Below are the five versions of his jab, each one tied to specific footwork. These aren't just punch variations — the feet are what make each one work. Drill each version separately before mixing them.

JAB 1 OF 5 · FOOTWORK The Step-In Jab
▾

The foundation. Leonard's default jab — thrown as he steps his lead foot slightly forward to close distance, creating momentum that adds snap without needing extra arm strength. Every other jab variation is a modification of this one.

FEET

  • Lead foot steps forward 3–4 inches at the same moment the jab leaves your hand. Not before, not after — together.
  • Back foot immediately follows, sliding up to restore stance width. If your back foot stays behind, your weight gets dumped forward and you're vulnerable.
  • After the jab lands: immediately circle left or reset — never stand directly in front of where the punch landed.

HANDS

  • Lead shoulder rotates forward, fist extends straight from the cheekbone to the target. Thumb turns down at full extension — your palm should face the floor when the punch connects.
  • Snap back to your guard the instant the fist makes contact. The power is in the snap, not a push.
  • Rear hand does not move at all during the jab. It stays at the cheekbone the whole time.

JAB 2 OF 5 · RHYTHM BREAKER The Triple Jab
▾

Leonard didn't throw the jab once and wait — he fired it in fast, back-to-back bursts. Three jabs in a row overwhelm an opponent's guard before they can reset, and the third jab often found a gap the first two created. Used to set up the right hand or a body attack.

FEET

  • First jab: small step in with the lead foot. Second jab: lead foot resets, another tiny advance. Third jab: same — three micro-steps forward, not one big lunge.
  • After the third jab, your feet should be in a slightly closer stance than where you started. That's intentional — you're already in range for the right hand.
  • Back foot keeps pace with each advance. If it lags, you've spread your feet too wide and lost your power base.

HANDS

  • Each jab snaps back fully before the next one goes out — don't leave your arm extended and flick from there, that's arm punching with no power.
  • Vary the speed within the three: throw the first at 70%, the second at 80%, the third at full — so the last one has genuine pop.
  • Rear hand stays absolutely still during all three jabs. It's loading, not resting.

JAB 3 OF 5 · ANGLE JAB The Circling Jab
▾

Leonard would launch the body jab usually as he circled opponents — because of his speed, he was able to control the distance in which he fought. The circling jab isn't just a punch thrown while moving — it's a punch whose angle changes because of where your feet are. You jab from a position your opponent didn't expect you to be in.

FEET

  • Circle left continuously — push off the right foot, step the left foot to the left, right foot follows. Keep moving in a smooth arc, not a stop-start shuffle.
  • Throw the jab at the moment your lead foot plants during the circle. Your body's rotation as you circle adds angle to the punch automatically — you're not punching straight ahead, you're punching from a slightly different line each time.
  • After the jab: continue circling in the same direction, don't stop and stand. The movement before AND after the punch is what keeps you safe.

HANDS

  • The jab fires on the same line as always — straight from the lead shoulder to the target. But because you're sideways to your opponent's line of attack, the angle of impact changes.
  • Keep the rear hand high while circling — if you drop it to "help" you move, you're exposed the instant the jab lands.
  • Short, sharp snap — circling jabs are not looping or wide. They're the same compact punch thrown from a moving base.

JAB 4 OF 5 · DECEPTION The Foot-Feint Jab Over Top
▾

Leonard would fake to the body with the same foot movement he used for the real body jab — and then instead unleash the right hand over the top, often catching his target as they blocked the midsection. The foot feint is the whole move. Everything else reacts to it.

FEET

  • Dip your knees slightly and step your lead foot forward exactly as you would for the real body jab. This is the feint — your opponent can't tell which punch is coming from the foot movement alone.
  • Instead of staying low, immediately straighten back up as you throw the real punch upstairs. The leg drive from coming back up actually powers the jab or right hand over the top.
  • Keep the foot movement small and quick — a big dramatic dip telegraphs that you're setting something up.

HANDS

  • During the feint: lead hand drops slightly toward the body line — not a full jab, just enough movement to get the reaction you need.
  • The real punch comes over the top as the opponent's guard drops to cover the fake body shot. Fire the right hand or a sharp jab straight to the chin.
  • Rear hand stays tight — the whole point is a quick deception and a clean counter. Don't load up.

JAB 5 OF 5 · DEFENSIVE JAB The Pull-Back Counter Jab
▾

Most fighters think of the jab as an attacking punch. Leonard also used it as a counter — pulling his head back just enough to let an incoming jab miss by an inch, then snapping his own jab back along exactly the same line before the opponent could recover. The incoming punch creates the opening; the pull-back jab fills it.

FEET

  • Weight shifts onto the back foot during the pull — not a step back, just a lean. You need both feet on the floor to fire back immediately.
  • The moment your own jab goes out, weight shifts forward again onto the front foot to add power to the counter.
  • Don't shuffle backward. The pull is from the waist and head only. If your feet move, you've taken yourself out of range to counter.

HANDS

  • Lean back just enough — maybe 3–4 inches — for the incoming punch to miss your chin. Too far and you're out of range to counter. Too little and you eat it.
  • Fire your jab the instant their arm is at full extension. That's when they're most committed and least able to defend. Your jab goes straight to their chin.
  • Rear hand stays at your cheekbone the whole time — don't drop it to help yourself lean back.

JAB TECHNIQUE TIPS — FIX THESE FIRST

  • ⚠ Dropping the rear hand — your rear hand must not move when you jab. If it drops, you're open for a counter right hand. Drill with your rear fist touching your cheek.
  • ⚠ Arm punching — if the power is coming only from your arm and shoulder, the jab has no snap. Your lead shoulder needs to rotate forward on every jab. Feel the push from the ball of your back foot.
  • ⚠ Not snapping back — leaving your arm extended after the jab is called arm-punching and telegraphs your next punch. The jab returns to your face as fast as it left.
  • ⚠ Standing flat after the jab — every jab should end with your feet moving. Either circle left, reset your angle, or set up your next punch while moving. Standing still is a target.
  • ⚠ Only jabbing to the head — Leonard used the body jab constantly to drain energy and pull the guard down. If you only go upstairs, your opponent only guards one place.
  • ⚠ Telegraphing with the shoulder — if your lead shoulder dips or rises before the jab goes out, a sharp opponent will time it. The jab should erupt from a neutral shoulder position.

SUGAR RAY LEONARD · MISDIRECTION The Bolo Punch
▾

Leonard's most famous trick wasn't really about power — it was theater with a purpose. He'd wind his rear hand up in a wide circular motion as if loading a huge looping shot, pulling the opponent's eyes and guard toward that hand. Then he'd snap a quick, ordinary jab in with the other hand, into a face that wasn't ready for it. He used exactly this against Roberto Duran in their 1980 rematch.

FEET

  • Stay in your normal stance through the whole motion — the wind-up is a hand trick, not a footwork commitment.
  • Weight stays centered; you need to fire the real punch the instant the wind-up has done its job.

HANDS

  • Wind the rear hand up and back in a slow, exaggerated circular motion — the bigger the wind-up, the more attention it pulls.
  • Snap the jab (or whatever punch you choose) with the OTHER hand the instant their eyes or guard shift toward the winding hand. Timing, not power, makes this work.

SUGAR RAY LEONARD · RANGE CONTROL The Body Jab Level-Change
▾

Leonard rarely jabbed to one spot. He'd work the head with quick jabs to keep an opponent's hands up high, then suddenly drop the same jab into the midsection — scoring points and draining energy at once, since body shots cost more stamina to absorb than anything upstairs.

FEET

  • Head jab: normal stance, weight even.
  • Body jab: bend both knees to drop your whole level — don't just bend at the waist. Staying upright while reaching down weakens the punch and exposes your chin.

HANDS

  • Throw 2–3 jabs upstairs first to get the opponent's hands and eyes high.
  • Drop levels with your legs, drive the jab into the midsection, then pop immediately back up. Rear hand stays up the entire time — you're most exposed while bent down.

SUGAR RAY LEONARD · ADAPTABILITY The Boxer-Puncher Switch
▾

Leonard's greatest weapon may have been his ability to change what kind of fighter he was mid-fight. Against Thomas Hearns, he spent early rounds boxing at range and falling behind on the cards — then switched into pressure mode, closing distance and hurting Hearns with a left hook in round six to turn the entire fight around. The skill isn't one punch, it's recognizing when pure boxing isn't working and having a second gear ready.

FEET

  • Boxing mode: stay light, circling, out of range, feet always resetting.
  • Power mode: plant heavier, square up slightly more to generate force — trading a step of range for the ability to load a real punch.

HANDS

  • Lead with a jab to close range safely, then immediately follow with a committed hook or cross — a full mode change in rhythm and intent, not a single trick punch.
  • Once in power mode, keep the rear hand tighter to the chin than usual — you're trading some defense for offense.

SUGAR RAY LEONARD · FIGHT-ENDER The Finishing Left Hook
▾

Leonard called his left hook against Dave "Boy" Green the hardest single punch he ever threw — a single shot that ended the fight in the fourth round. It wasn't a wild swing; it came off the same fast hands and footwork as everything else in his arsenal, just committed fully instead of held back.

FEET

  • Same base stance, but as you commit, pivot the rear foot inward and drive your weight fully onto the front foot — this is thrown with total commitment, not measured speed.
  • Stay square enough that your hips can rotate fully; too bladed a stance robs you of the rotation.

HANDS

  • Set it up off a jab or a feint — it works because the opponent isn't expecting full commitment from a fighter known for speed.
  • Elbow bent around 90°, fist travels in a short, tight arc. Rear hand glued to the chin through the whole punch — full commitment means zero margin for a dropped guard.

SUGAR RAY LEONARD · ROUND-STEALER The Showtime Flurry
▾

Late against Marvin Hagler, an exhausted Leonard found something extra — answering every one of Hagler's punches with a faster, flashier flurry of his own, landing fewer shots but winning the room and the judges with sheer output in the final rounds. A championship-rounds weapon: when you're tired and behind, volume and energy can still steal a round.

FEET

  • Stay in tight, controlled range — this isn't new footwork, it's maximum output from wherever you already are.
  • Brace through both feet for a short, intense burst rather than conserving for a longer exchange.

HANDS

  • Throw 6–10 punches in a continuous burst, prioritizing speed and volume over individual power.
  • Mix head and body freely within the flurry, then reset your guard fully the instant it ends — a flurry that ends with your hands down undoes the whole point.

FOLDER 3 — DURAN

Roberto Duran

"Manos de Piedra" (Hands of Stone) · Four-Division World Champion

If Leonard's game was about avoiding a fight, Duran's was about making sure there wasn't one to avoid. He built a four-division championship career on relentless forward pressure, smothering infighting, and a body attack that broke opponents down over twelve rounds. Underneath the brawler reputation was real craft — feints that drew mistakes, footwork that cut off the ring before a punch landed, and defensive instincts sharp enough to keep him near the top for almost two decades. This folder is about closing distance and making opponents pay for being caught there.

BASE STANCE — THE PRESSURE FIGHTER

FOOT POSITION — TOP VIEW

GUARD — FRONT VIEW

FEET

  • Narrower, more compact stance than a typical boxer's — short, controlled steps rather than long ones, built to close distance without giving an opponent room to circle away.
  • Weight rides slightly forward, on the front half of both feet — built for moving in, not retreating.
  • Rear foot drives every forward step. You rarely back up, so the back foot is always loading the next advance, not preparing an exit.

HANDS

  • Guard sits a little tighter and lower than a pure outside boxer's — built for slipping and rolling at close range, not just blocking from distance.
  • Hands stay active and circling rather than static, ready to parry, smother, or control an opponent's arm the instant range closes.
  • Chin tucked low behind the lead shoulder, head always moving slightly — never a still target, even while pressing forward.

ROBERTO DURAN · INFIGHT CONTROL The Circling Hands Hold
▾

Instead of snapping every punch straight back to his guard, Duran would sometimes circle his hand over or under an opponent's guard and into a controlling hold — blending striking and grappling into one motion. It let him smother an opponent's offense from the inside while setting up his own follow-up shots.

FEET

  • Requires being already at very close range — short, controlled steps to close the gap fully first, not from outside.
  • Weight centered and low, knees bent. This is a balance-heavy technique; you need a stable base to control someone else's arm.

HANDS

  • After landing or throwing a punch, instead of retracting straight back, let that hand circle over (or under) the opponent's lead arm.
  • Use it to press or trap their arm for a beat, taking away their ability to punch back, while the other hand stays free for a follow-up.

ROBERTO DURAN · ENTRY COMBO The Shifting One-Two
▾

One of Duran's favorite ways to get from the outside to the inside — a one-two where the cross doesn't travel in a straight line. It winds around the opponent's lead arm, sneaking inside their guard rather than going through it, so he's already at close range by the time the combination finishes.

FEET

  • Step forward with the jab as usual, but on the cross, step the rear foot further forward than normal — you're walking into range, not just punching.
  • By the end of the combination your feet should already be in close-range position.

HANDS

  • Jab as the opening shot, same as always.
  • On the cross, let the punch curve slightly around the outside of the opponent's lead arm — aiming to finish close to or past their guard rather than through the front of it.

ROBERTO DURAN · PRESSURE FOOTWORK Cutting Off the Ring
▾

Duran almost never chased an opponent in a straight line — he cut angles to close distance, taking away the ring a step at a time, so an opponent trying to circle away kept running into him instead of escaping.

FEET

  • Instead of moving straight toward an opponent, step diagonally to intercept the direction they're circling — if they move right, cut that path off rather than following straight behind them.
  • Keep steps short and controlled. Overcommitting on one big step is how pressure fighters get countered.

HANDS

  • Keep the guard tight and high while cutting angles — you're positioning, not punching, for most of this movement.
  • Once the angle is cut off and the opponent has nowhere to circle, that's when the offense starts.

ROBERTO DURAN · BODY ATTACK The Liver Attack
▾

Once Duran got to close range, the body became a primary target — short, brutal hooks delivered while tying up an opponent's offense, designed to wear down the legs and the will over a fight rather than land one big shot.

FEET

  • Stay low and centered at close range — bend the knees rather than bending at the waist to reach the body.
  • Weight balanced between both feet so you can fire either hand without telegraphing which one.

HANDS

  • Keep both hands active and close to the body at this range — short, compact hooks, since there's no room for a long punch up close.
  • Aim for the lower ribs and liver (opponent's right side, your left hand). Keep your head moving the entire time you work the body.

ROBERTO DURAN · COUNTER PUNCHING Feint-and-Counter Pressure
▾

Duran's aggression was rarely careless. He'd move forward throwing feints, draw an opponent into throwing first, and then capitalize on the opening — using pressure as the setup for a counter rather than a wild brawl.

FEET

  • Advance steadily, same pressure footwork as cutting off the ring, but stay ready to plant and load a counter the instant an opponent commits to a punch.
  • Don't lunge during the feint phase — small advances only, base ready to counter at any moment.

HANDS

  • Throw a feint (a partial jab, a shoulder dip) and read the reaction — do they flinch, throw back, cover up?
  • The moment they commit to their own punch, counter immediately into the opening it created. Rear hand stays home throughout the feinting phase.
